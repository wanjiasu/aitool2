Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)

In simple terms: Imagine an art forger (the "Generator") trying to create a fake Picasso, and an art detective (the "Discriminator") trying to spot the fake. The forger keeps getting better at faking, and the detective keeps getting better at spotting fakes. Eventually, the forger becomes so good that the detective can't tell the difference. That's a GAN, used to create new, realistic data.

Detailed Explanation

A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) is a class of machine learning frameworks designed by Ian Goodfellow and his colleagues in 2014. It consists of two neural networks, the Generator and the Discriminator, which are trained simultaneously in a zero-sum game.

The Generator's job is to create new data instances (like images, music, or text) that mimic a real dataset. The Discriminator's job is to evaluate this data and determine whether it is real (from the original dataset) or fake (created by the Generator). The two networks are in constant competition, which pushes both to improve until the Generator can create synthetic data that is nearly indistinguishable from real data.

Example of Use

One of the most famous applications of GANs is creating hyper-realistic but entirely fake human faces, as seen on websites like "This Person Does Not Exist". They are also used in fashion design to create new clothing styles, in video games to generate realistic textures, and in medicine to synthesize medical images for training purposes.
